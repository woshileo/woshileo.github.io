The Month Between Jobs

One resignation letter, one start date, and twenty-nine days in the gap — Tokyo, Bangkok, a Muay Thai camp in Phuket, then Barcelona, Madrid, and Lisbon before the new badge photo.

There is exactly one moment in adult life when nobody can email you: the gap between jobs. I had a month of it, so we spent it crossing the planet the long way — a week eating through Tokyo, a week in Thailand that ended with me signing into a fight camp, and then, still sore, a ten-day Iberian victory lap from Barcelona to Lisbon. Six countries, three Muay Thai gyms, two Gaudí houses, one Grand Seiko. Here's how it went.

Chapter 01

Tokyo, unemployed and thriving

April 27 – May 3 · based in Kyobashi/Ginza
Day one · Oji → Nishi-Shinjuku

A streetcar and a sushi counter

First full day: rode the Toden Arakawa Line — Tokyo's last surviving streetcar — standing right behind the driver's cab from Oji down through Minamiotsuka. That night, an intimate omakase counter in Nishi-Shinjuku, Hibiki and Yamazaki bottles lined up behind the itamae.

Nakano Broadway

Junkissa Zingaro

Takashi Murakami's Shōwa-retro kissaten on the second floor of Nakano Broadway — a ceiling full of colored glass globes, a disco ball, a Space Invaders mural, and game-machine tables. Maximalist nostalgia done by an artist with a budget.

Shōwa Day · Nakameguro

A festival we didn't plan

Walked into a neighborhood matsuri on Shōwa Day — crews in matching happi coats staging Edo-style fire-brigade ladder stunts. Recovered with a pour-over at ONIBUS Coffee, the little house by the train tracks that every coffee person tells you about, because they're right.

Kyobashi · the splurge

The severance watch

Some people mark a job change with a tattoo. I walked into a Ginza boutique and walked out with a Grand Seiko. Unboxed on the hotel bed at 9am with the gravity the moment deserved. No regrets, then or now.

Ginza

Beans roasted to order, Ginza in the rain

Found a roast-to-order bean shop in east Ginza — you pick from bins of green coffee, choose one of ten roast levels, and they fire it while you wait. Last evening: the Ginza 4-chōme crossing in the rain, Wako clock tower glowing, before the Tokyo Station crowds and a night flight south.

Chapter 02

Bangkok: coffee, wine & the first gym

May 4 – 7 · based on Soi Sathon 12
King Power Mahanakhon

Pad thai under the pixel tower

Up to Thailand's glitchy pixelated skyscraper, then lunch in its food hall at Pad Thai Fai Ta Lu — "pad thai of the piercing flame" — where the wok fire does most of the talking. Char on the noodles like it's a steak.

Soi Ari 4

NANA Coffee Roasters, Ari

Bangkok's specialty-coffee scene is operating at a different difficulty level. At NANA's garden flagship in Ari, the Costa Rica filter arrives in a wine glass on a carved wooden world map, with a tasting card. Brunch was excellent; the theater was better.

Bang Kho Laem

PK.Saenchai Muay Thai Gym

The first of the trip's three gyms — the camp associated with Saenchai, the greatest of his generation. Two afternoons of pad rounds in industrial-strength humidity while actual pros sparred one ring over. A useful dose of perspective before Phuket.

Soi Sathon 12

Mod Kaew Wine Bar

Two minutes from our place: Mod Kaew, ranked Thailand's #1 wine list that year, all low-intervention bottles at almost-retail markups. The neon over the dining room — "More skin contact than a nudist colony" — remains the best wine joke I've seen on a wall.

Chapter 03

Phuket: fight camp

May 8 – 15 · Soi Ta-iad, Chalong
Fitness Street

Signing into Tiger Muay Thai

Landed at noon, signed the Tiger Muay Thai paperwork by 2:48pm — the metadata does not lie. Soi Ta-iad is a one-mile street that is entirely gyms, protein cafés, and fighters walking around with hand wraps. As an environment for someone with a month and no meetings, it is dangerously effective.

Old Phuket Town

Rest-day pilgrimage

Day two's recovery plan: khanom jeen — nests of fresh rice noodles drowned in crab curry, Phuket's definitive breakfast — at a local shop in Old Town, then an iced americano at MONDO on Thalang Road among the Sino-Portuguese shophouses. Evening scooter run to Patong to confirm it's exactly what everyone says it is.

May 11–15

The week with no photos

Strength work at Kong Fitness, Muay Thai at Tiger, and then — five straight days with zero pictures on the camera roll. That's not a gap in the record; that is the record. Train, eat, sleep, repeat, supervised by the guesthouse cat. The single most effective vacation week I've ever had.

Chapter 04

Barcelona: Gaudí on both ends

May 16 – 18 · based in the Eixample
Passeig de Gràcia

Casa Batlló, straight off the plane

Eleven time zones from Phuket and into Gaudí's dragon house by 7pm — bone balconies, the blue-tiled lightwell, and the new immersive digital cube at the end, which is what happens when you let an AI artist loose on Gaudí's geometry.

El Call · Carrer de Casp

Brugarol, then 1,600 whiskies

Dinner at Brugarol in the old Jewish Quarter — a Japanese-Catalan tasting menu plated on stone, beet petals under elderflower, dessert served in eggshells. Nightcap at La Whiskeria (since 1969), whose back wall holds sixteen hundred bottles and whose bartenders treat a midnight cappuccino request with total grace.

Gràcia

Casa Vicens

Gaudí's first house — the one he built at 31, before the curves, all Moorish tile and green latticework. Quieter than anything on Passeig de Gràcia and the better visit for it.

Eixample · El Born

Vermouth hour, properly observed

Lunch at La Bodegueta (a cellar since 1942): fuet, anchovies in olive oil, pa amb tomàquet on a marble table. Then across town to Bar Brutal, the natural-wine pioneer of El Born, for coppa and a glass of something cloudy under the mounted marlin.

Carrer de Provença

Gresca

Rafa Peña's bistronomic standby for the last Barcelona dinner — red mullet over smoked eggplant, cod with potatoes, a lacquered sesame-glazed rib. The kind of restaurant chefs name when you ask where they actually eat.

Chapter 05

Madrid & Toledo: museums by day, jazz by night

May 18 – 22 · AVE from Barcelona · based in Malasaña
Paseo del Prado · Las Letras

The Prado, then a pulga

Morning with Velázquez and Goya at the Prado, then lunch at Bar Neila — an old-school joint whose hand-painted blue sign promises baguettes, perritos, sandwiches, pulgas and delivers on all counts.

Plaza de Oriente

The royal quarter

Plaza de Oriente, the Royal Palace, and the Campo del Moro gardens below it — the full Habsburg-to-Bourbon promenade in one golden afternoon.

Plazuela de San Ginés

Two dinners, zero shame

Hand-pulled noodles on Calle de Silva (with the obligatory photo at the viral "Te extraño mucho en Madrid / 我在马德里很想你" sign), followed forty minutes later by churros con chocolate at San Ginés, frying since 1894. Madrid respects a double-dinner strategy.

Day trip · 33 minutes by AVE

Toledo

The Alcázar rising over the Tagus, a maze of old-town lanes, and — in a small church called Santo Tomé — El Greco's Burial of the Count of Orgaz, still hanging in the exact spot it was painted for in 1586. We closed with a riverside walk and a pincho moruno back in Madrid, in a tavern with sherry barrels stacked behind the bar.

Plaza del Ángel · last night

Guernica, then Café Central

Guernica at the Reina Sofía in the late-day quiet, then dinner and a sax-and-piano set at Café Central, the art-deco jazz room that anchored Plaza del Ángel for 43 years. Two months after our visit it announced it was closing. Sometimes the timing gods just hand you one.

Chapter 06

Lisbon & Sintra: the final descent

May 22 – 25 · Hotel do Chiado, Baixa
Baixa · Alfama

Nata to castle in ninety minutes

Off the plane, first pastel de nata at Manteigaria on Rua Augusta — eaten warm on the calçada within six minutes, per the photo timestamps — then up to Castelo de São Jorge, where the peacocks run the place and the whole city lays itself out below.

Portas do Sol · Chiado

Trams below, rooftop above

Down through Alfama past the yellow Tram 28 at Portas do Sol, then sunset drinks at our hotel's rooftop bar with Baixa's rooftops running down to the Tagus. Lisbon does golden hour like it invented it.

Day trip · Sintra

Pena Palace

The red-and-yellow fever dream on the hilltop, then the walk down through the forest of Parque Natural de Sintra-Cascais with the Moorish Castle ramparts across the valley. Refueled in town with a box of pastries crowned by a travesseiro — Sintra's warm, sugared almond pillow.

Belém

Jerónimos & the riverside

Last full day: the Manueline cloisters of the Jerónimos Monastery — stone carved like rope and coral — then the riverside walk with the 25 de Abril bridge doing its Golden Gate impression. Souvenir run at Silva & Feijóo, the old-world grocery on Rua dos Bacalhoeiros; several tins of fish made the flight home.

Campo de Santa Clara · final night

A fado farewell

Last dinner at O Corrido, a casa de fado beside the National Pantheon: amêijoas à Bulhão Pato, octopus salad, and sets of live fado — singer, Portuguese guitarra, classical guitar — until past eleven. The right way to end a month like this: somebody singing beautifully about longing, the night before you fly home to a new job.

What we ate

A month is long enough to eat across three food cultures properly — fish-sauce, olive-oil, and butter. The named spots are confirmed from signage in the photos or exact addresses; a few neighborhood places stay anonymous because their signs didn't make the frame.

Barcelona · El Call

Brugarol

Japanese-meets-Catalan tasting menu in the old Jewish Quarter — plated on stone, finished in eggshells.

Barcelona · Eixample

Gresca

Rafa Peña's bistronomy: red mullet over smoked eggplant. A chef's-chef restaurant.

Madrid · Chueca

NaDo

Iván Domínguez's Galician haute cuisine. The dessert arrives buried under a snowbank of shaved cheese.

Madrid · Malasaña

Picholeiros

Galician tapas for the arrival meal: an anchovy lattice over tomato, ensaladilla on toast.

Lisbon · Cais do Sodré

Sala de Corte

Chef Luís Gaspar's steakhouse — dry-aged ribeye out of the Josper charcoal oven, flor de sal on top.

Bangkok · Sukhumvit 33

Home-style Thai

Tom kha, stir-fried octopus, green beans on floral enamelware — the dinner that tasted most like somebody's house.

Phuket · Old Town

Khanom jeen, crab curry

Rice-noodle nests and nam ya pu — Phuket's breakfast of champions, fight-camp approved.

Madrid · San Ginés

Chocolatería San Ginés

Churros and drinking chocolate, continuously since 1894. Ordered as a second dinner without hesitation.

Madrid · Malasaña

The sherry-barrel tavern

PX, oloroso, and palo cortado barrels behind the bar; a pincho moruno the length of the table in front of it.

Lisbon · Rua Augusta

Manteigaria

The pastel de nata benchmark — blistered tops, molten custard, eaten warm on the street.

Sintra · Praça da República

Travesseiro

Sintra's sugared almond pillow, invented down the street in 1862 — inhaled on a bench on the square.

Bangkok · Langsuan

The red-velvet nightcap

A moody Paris-brasserie of a bar inside Velaa Sindhorn Village — last drink of the first Bangkok night.
