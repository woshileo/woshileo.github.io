Credit Card Churning Guide

Written By: Leo Siow

Originally written May 16th, 2021 · Updated June 7th, 2026

Introduction

I am writing this guide in hopes of introducing beginners to the world of credit cards. By entering this hobby, you will be able to travel hack and maximize cash back on your everyday purchases. You may be asking, “What is travel hacking?” Here is the answer to that question: Travel hacking is the art of traveling for free by utilizing credit card sign-up bonuses and rewards.

Wow! That sounds great right? However, you may be wondering if there’s a catch to it. If you’re a responsible credit card user, then no, not at all. However, if you are NOT a responsible credit card user, close this document immediately. Let me explain why you shouldn’t play this game if you suck at managing your credit card expenses: Credit card issuers make the bulk of their revenue from charging interest on statement balances. This means that individuals who don’t pay off their balance in full or just pay the minimum required are the biggest cash cows.

Achieving sign-up bonuses often require you to spend X amount of dollars in a set amount of time. The minimum spend required may be large at times, and because of this it encourages consumers to spend more money. Sometimes consumers dig themselves into a hole that they can’t get out because of this. I will be explaining ways you can meet this minimum spend required (i.e. paying rent), but if you are spending money you wouldn’t have been spending in the first place just to meet this minimum spend requirement, then it really isn’t worth it. That’s pretty much it! As long as you don’t do the above, you can successfully play the travel hacking game and explore the world completely free.

So, if you’re ready to jump into the wonderful world of travel hacking with credit card points and maximizing your cash back, let’s jump into it!

What's Changed Since 2021

I originally wrote this guide in 2021, and the hobby has shifted quite a bit since then. The core strategy still holds, but here's what's different in 2026 and worth keeping in mind as you read the rest of this guide:

  • Premium annual fees have ballooned. The flagship premium cards (Amex Platinum, Chase Sapphire Reserve, etc.) have moved to a "credits/coupon-book" model with much higher annual fees offset by a pile of statement credits. They can still be worth it, but only if you'll actually use the credits — otherwise the math gets ugly fast.
  • Points devalue over time. Transfer partners and award charts get worse, not better. Earn points to use them on a plan you have in mind, rather than hoarding them for years.
  • Manufactured spending is harder and riskier. A lot of the old reliable methods have died or been clamped down on. I treat MS as a niche tool now, not the foundation of a strategy.
  • More flexible-points currencies exist. It's no longer just Chase and Amex. Capital One and Citi now have genuinely competitive transferable-points ecosystems, which gives you a lot more room after you've exhausted Chase.
  • Paying rent for points is now mainstream. Cards like Bilt let you earn points on rent with no processing fee, which used to require workarounds.

Rules

First and foremost, there are a number of rules that you need to follow when “churning” credit cards. “Churning” is the process of signing up for credit cards, using the sign-up bonuses, then cancelling the card. The reason why there are a variety of rules to follow is because each credit card issuer has their own guidelines when evaluating risk in applicants. Banks generally don’t like to extend too much credit to someone who has irresponsible spending habits. In addition, consumers who are seeking credit multiple times in a short period are also seen as risky. I will go into more details of the aforementioned topics later, but as long as you stick to these rules and follow my recommendations, you should be good to go.

I always recommend people to start with Chase credit cards first, and there are many good reasons for that. Chase credit cards generally offer the best rewards and bonuses in the industry alongside American Express. These two credit card issuers blow the rest of the industry away. In addition, Chase Ultimate Reward (UR) points and Amex Membership Reward (MR) points are some of the most lucrative currencies in the market. With that being said, I would start with Chase over American Express (Amex). This is because of Chase’s 5/24 rule, and also because of Amex’s once-in-a-lifetime rule.

What is the Chase 5/24 rule?

This is one of the most famous rules in the credit card churning game. Basically, Chase only accepts applicants who have opened less than 5 personal credit cards in the last 24 months. Otherwise, applicants will be auto-denied. As of 2026 this rule is still very much alive and strictly enforced — plan as if you'll be auto-denied over 5/24, and treat any approval over the limit as a pleasant surprise.

One nice change: in June 2025 Chase eliminated the old Sapphire 48-month rule (the restriction that you couldn't earn a Sapphire bonus within 48 months of the last one, and could only hold one Sapphire card at a time). That restriction is gone, which gives you more flexibility around the Sapphire family than I had when I first wrote this.

If you don’t have a year’s worth of credit history, then skip to the next section. If you fulfill the guidelines I listed, then you can follow this roadmap:

This roadmap is my ideal route to take, as I get the opportunity to receive a lot of travel points while also maximizing my cash back. I highly recommend you to spread out your credit card applications by at least three months. This is because Chase has some more rules regarding velocity (the rate at which you apply for cards) and also applicants who seek credit too quickly are usually denied.

Chase velocity rules

  • 1/30: You can only apply for a business card once every 30 days.
  • 2/30: You can only apply for two Chase cards (1 business and 1 personal) every 30 days.

Speaking of business cards… Why should you apply for them?

I get this question a lot, and many people are afraid to apply for business cards. Well I’m here to tell you that you shouldn’t worry at all. If you drive for Uber/Lyft, sell items on Ebay, fundraise for a club, or exchange any other types of goods for money, awesome! You have a business. I’ll be explaining how to apply to business cards in a later section.

I highly recommend you to apply for business cards for these reasons:

  • They offer JUICY sign-up bonuses and great reward redemptions! The Chase Ink Business family (Preferred, Cash, Unlimited, Premier) consistently has some of the best business bonuses out there.
  • They don’t show up on your personal credit report. This means that when you apply for them, they don’t count against your 5/24. Instead, they report to a seperate business credit bureau. However, do note that you still have to be under 5/24 to apply for Chase business cards.

How do I fill out the credit card application?

For personal cards, it’s pretty straightforward. The only thing I recommend is that for the “annual income” field, you should think more broadly. Your annual income can refer to your entire household income, or it can be the income listed on your offer letter even if you haven’t started working yet. Obviously don’t lie/put extremely high numbers, but do know you have room to maneuver.

For business cards it’s pretty similar to filling out personal card applications. The only difference is additional fields for “business revenue”, “EIN”, “business name” and “years in business”. If you have a business that has reported income on your tax returns, you can just fill out these fields accordingly. If you have a “business” that doesn’t have reported taxes or isn’t incorporated, I recommend you to put 0 for “years in business” and a lower number (i.e. 1k) for “business revenue”. For “EIN” and “business name”, if you aren’t an incorporated business like an LLC, you can just apply as a sole proprietorship. This means you can simply put your social security number in the “EIN” field and your full name in “business name”. That’s it!

I don’t want to pay the annual fee...

You probably also have concerns with the annual fees. Well, this is how I think of it: on the mid-tier cards, you’re typically paying around $95 in return for several hundred dollars of value in the first year. Not a bad rate of return right? A lot of the cards I listed are keeper cards, meaning they are worth keeping for the long term because of the value they offer.

A 2026 caveat on the premium cards: the flagship cards (Amex Platinum, Chase Sapphire Reserve, etc.) have pushed their annual fees way up — the Amex Platinum is now $895 as of January 2026 — and offset them with a long list of statement credits (hotel, dining, rideshare, streaming, etc.). These cards can absolutely still come out ahead, but only if you'll actually use those credits. If you wouldn't have spent that money anyway, mentally discount the credits to zero and re-do the math.

If you decide later on you do not like one of these annual fee cards, many of them offer a downgrade path to a no annual fee card. This is great because it will have no effect on your credit score and preserves your relationship with the issuer. Before you cancel or downgrade, always call the retention line first — issuers will frequently offer a statement credit or bonus points to keep you, which can make holding the card worth it for another year (see the retention section below). If you do want to cancel, you easily can do it after 11 months and before you’re charged for the next year!

How the heck do I meet the spending requirements (MSR)?

Some of the minimum spending requirements to get the sign-up bonuses may seem high! The first and best lever is to time your applications around large planned expenses — tuition, a security deposit, insurance premiums, travel you were going to book anyway. Put real, already-planned spending on the new card and the MSR takes care of itself.

Rent is usually the biggest monthly expense, and these days you can often put it on a card directly. Bilt lets you earn points on rent with no processing fee, which is a great way to chip away at an MSR every month. (Bilt overhauled its program in 2025, so check the current terms and earning structure before you rely on it.)

There's also “manufactured spending” (MS) — using services to turn a credit card charge into something that helps you hit your spend. A long-running example is Plastiq, which charges your credit card (currently around a ~3% processing fee) to cut a check or send a payment to a biller who doesn't normally take cards. It still works, but the fee means it only makes sense for the welcome bonus, not for ongoing spend. Be careful here: MS methods change and die frequently, fees eat into your returns, and aggressive MS can get your accounts shut down. I treat it as an occasional tool to close a gap on an MSR, not as a strategy. Don't go chasing methods you read about — verify they still work and understand the risk before putting money through them.

What if I don’t have a previous relationship with Chase?

Do you have a credit score above 700+ and more than a year’s worth of credit history?

If yes, then:

  • You have multiple options. You can still follow the roadmap above if you have a strong credit profile (more than 2 years). Otherwise:
  • You should apply for the Chase Freedom Flex / Freedom Unlimited. These are Chase starter cards that offer a lot of value and have great rewards, and they typically come with an easy cash-back sign-up bonus.
  • I recommend the Freedom/Freedom Unlimited because this will help you kickstart your relationship with Chase. Chase is far more likely to issue these cards, because they use it as a “test” card to see if you make payments on time / you are responsible / etc. Think of it as Chase testing the waters with you for a year. After a year of using this card, you should be ready to pursue higher tier cards with Chase.

If not, then:

  • You should open a checking account/savings account with Chase online or in-branch to start a relationship with them. Opening these types of accounts do not involve credit scores/checks. Plus, you often get a cash bonus depending on what type of accounts you open and how much you deposit.
  • You should apply for a Discover It card to build up your credit. Discover accepts most people even if they have a thin credit file. They also have a great first-year bonus in which they double the cash back you’ve earned in the first year.

Transferable Points: The Four Currencies

The whole reason to play this game with travel cards is "transferable points" — flexible currencies you can move to airline and hotel partners for outsized value, rather than redeeming at a fixed cash rate. In 2026 there are four major issuers with their own flexible currency, and a healthy strategy collects from more than one:

  • Chase Ultimate Rewards (UR) — the best all-around ecosystem for most people, with strong partners like Hyatt, United, and Air Canada. Start here.
  • American Express Membership Rewards (MR) — deep airline transfer partner list, especially for international premium-cabin redemptions.
  • Capital One Miles — has grown into a serious player with 20+ transfer partners and simple, beginner-friendly mechanics.
  • Citi ThankYou Points — strong bonus categories and a solid partner list, including a 1:1 transfer to American AAdvantage.

You don't need all four, but once you've worked through Chase it's worth diversifying so you're not exposed to a single program devaluing.

American Express Credit Cards

American Express has long had some of the best credit card offers. There are a few quirks to Amex's rules that you need to understand, because they're stricter about welcome-offer eligibility than most issuers.

What is the Amex once-in-a-lifetime rule?

Amex's official policy is that you're only eligible for a welcome bonus on a given card "once per lifetime." In practice, "lifetime" tends to mean roughly 7 years on most products, and Amex doesn't publish the exact rules. This is the main reason I save Amex for after Chase — you want to use each Amex bonus deliberately, since you (usually) only get it once.

What is Amex "pop-up jail"?

When you apply for an Amex card, you may get a pop-up before Amex checks your credit telling you that you won't be eligible for the welcome bonus on this card. This is informally called "pop-up jail." Amex uses it to screen people they think are churning. If you see it, don't proceed — there's no point taking the credit inquiry for no bonus. People generally get out of pop-up jail over time by slowing down applications and putting genuine spend on their existing Amex cards.

Charge Cards vs Credit Cards

American Express is one of the few banks in the United States that offer charge card products. Amex charge card products include the Gold and Platinum cards. Charge cards don't have a pre-defined spending limit like credit cards, because the full balance on the charge card must be paid off by the end of every statement. Functionally, the way I use charge cards and credit cards are the same, because I pay off all my balances immediately.

4 Card Rule

You can only have four personal Amex credit cards at any time. This limit does not include business cards. This limit does not include charge cards like the Amex Gold and Platinum.

1 Card Every 5 Days

American Express will only approve one card for you every five days.

2 Card Every 90 Days

American Express will only approve at most two cards for you every 90 days.

A note on Amex annual fees in 2026

Amex's premium cards have gotten expensive. The Platinum is now $895/year (as of January 2026) and the Gold is $325/year. Both are offset by a stack of statement credits, but those credits come as a "coupon book" of monthly/quarterly allotments at specific merchants. They can be worth it, but only if those merchants fit your life. Read the credit list carefully before committing.

Retention Offers

One of the most underrated parts of this hobby: before you cancel or downgrade any annual-fee card, call the issuer's retention line and ask if there are any offers for keeping the card. Issuers would much rather give you a statement credit or some bonus points than lose you entirely. A typical retention offer might be a credit that covers most or all of the annual fee, or a chunk of points in exchange for some spend.

It costs you a five-minute phone call. If you get a good offer, you keep a card you like for another year at little or no cost. If you don't, you can still cancel or downgrade as planned. There's almost no downside to asking.
